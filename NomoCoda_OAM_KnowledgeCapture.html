Architecture Flow Knowledge Capture

What OAM Knows & How It Learns

Three categories of knowledge live in Organizational AI Memory. Each is captured differently — from fully automated system observation to natural human workflow interactions.

Three Categories of Knowledge

All three live in OAM. The first two can be largely automated. The third requires humans — and it's the most valuable.

📡

Operational Signals

What Happened

Observable facts. Timestamped, system-generated events captured from connected tools and agents. Objective and automated.

Examples: Vendor X was late. Budget trending 12% over. Task velocity dropped 20%. Client didn't respond to last two check-ins. New hire started Monday.
Captured by: Agents monitoring systems
🔄

Patterns

What Keeps Happening

Emergent intelligence visible only when signals accumulate over time and across systems. No single signal reveals a pattern. This is where compounding lives.

Examples: Vendor late 3x in 90 days across two projects. Client engagement drops 30 days before churn. Onboarding 2+ people in a month dips velocity for 3 weeks.
Captured by: System synthesis over time
🧠

Decision Context

Why It Happened & How People Think

The reasoning behind human decisions. Institutional logic, tradeoffs, and frameworks that walk out the door when someone leaves. The most valuable and hardest to capture.

Examples: Delayed project because mid-project onboarding slows teams short-term. Approved overage because client relationship is strategic. Restructured approvals because undocumented bottleneck existed.
Captured by: Human workflow interactions
How human signals get captured

Four Layers of Human Capture

The goal: make capture invisible. People don't change how they work. The system gets smarter by learning from what they're already doing.

Layer 1
Passive Capture

From existing behavior — zero extra effort

Agents monitor the systems people already work in and extract contextual signals from human-generated content. The human didn't do anything extra. They just did their job.

PM writes in Jira: "Moved deadline because client shifted priorities to Q2 launch." · Sales rep logs in HubSpot: "Client mentioned budget pressure from new CFO — renewal timing may shift." · Ops leader in Slack: "Holding off on vendor switch until after compliance audit."
Jira comments CRM notes Slack decisions Email context Report edits
Layer 2
Structured AI Interactions

Reasoning captured through natural AI conversations

When someone works through a problem with an AI agent — exploring options, weighing tradeoffs, arriving at a decision — the conversation itself becomes organizational memory. Not just the outcome, but the logic behind it.

A 30-minute conversation where an ops leader works through staffing options with an AI agent. The outcome: "Chose Option B." The captured context: "Chose Option B because client relationship matters more than short-term budget hit, and historically prioritizing budget over relationship with this client led to escalation."
AI agent conversations Decision reasoning Tradeoff analysis
Layer 3
Lightweight Prompts

One question at the moment of decision — not a form

At key decision points — approving a budget, closing a deal, completing a phase — the system asks one simple question. Captured at the moment of the decision, not as a retrospective exercise nobody wants to do.

"You approved the overage on Project Alpha. Anything the team should know about why?" → A single sentence response becomes rich decision context. Takes seconds, not minutes.
Budget approvals Deal closures Phase completions Escalation decisions
Layer 4
Partner Calibration

NomoCoda as context architect and validator — not daily capturer

The partner isn't in every meeting. They're not the bottleneck for daily capture. Layers 1–3 handle that. The partner's role is designing the capture logic, validating what the system learned, correcting misinterpretations, and injecting strategic context no system can detect.

"We just lost our biggest account." · "New VP wants reports structured differently." · "We're switching accounting systems next quarter." · Reviewing automated captures: "The system logged this as a negative signal, but the team actually sees this as a positive — correct the interpretation."
Strategic context Memory validation Capture design Monthly/quarterly

From Signals to Unified Intelligence

All three knowledge categories — operational signals, patterns, and decision context — flow into the synthesis layer and become a unified organizational understanding. When anything flows out, it draws from the whole picture, not individual fragments. The VP gets an intelligence brief, not 27 raw data points — with citations back to the contextual moments that informed it.

The Design Principle

The best organizational memory system is one that people don't even realize they're feeding — because they're just doing their jobs and the system is smart enough to learn from it. Layers 1–3 handle daily capture at scale. Layer 4 ensures the quality, accuracy, and strategic depth of what's being captured.
