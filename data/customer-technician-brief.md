# Customer Technician, Intelligence Brief

> Phase A, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Customers Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Customer Technician is the implementation engine of the CS organization. Where the Customer Advocate owns the ongoing relationship and the Customer Operator owns the infrastructure those relationships run on, the Customer Technician owns the most time-sensitive window in the customer lifecycle: the stretch from contract signature to first confirmed value. Their accountability is measured in go-live velocity, time-to-first-value, stakeholder activation breadth, and the technical quality of the handoff they deliver to the CSM once an implementation is live.

This is the Implementation Manager, the Professional Services Engineer, the Onboarding Specialist with a project plan and a technical integration checklist. They run kickoff calls, manage configuration sign-off sequences, track integration milestone completion, and push every technical blocker until it resolves. A good week is a mid-market implementation that cleared go-live six days ahead of plan because the pre-kickoff configuration template was complete and the right stakeholders were named on day one. A bad week is discovering that a Tier-2 account has stalled in configuration for three weeks because the AE didn't capture the use-case details at close, and now the Implementation Manager is rebuilding the scope from scratch in the first month of the engagement.

The Customer Technician lives in project management tools, configuration consoles, integration logs, and the handoff record that the AE left behind. They own the implementation timeline, the stakeholder activation plan, the integration quality gate, and the structured handoff brief that the CSM receives at go-live. They are the person whose work determines whether the customer reaches first value in 14 days or 60.

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Implementation Manager
- Onboarding Manager
- Professional Services Engineer
- Technical Onboarding Specialist
- Solutions Delivery Manager

**Seniority variations:**
- 50-150 employees: Often a single Implementation Manager who runs every onboarding alongside informal CSM duties; configuration scope and stakeholder management are owned by the same person
- 150-350 employees: Implementation team of 2-5 with a lead or manager; dedicated ownership of the onboarding timeline and go-live checklist; CSM handoff is a distinct step
- 350-500 employees: Director of Professional Services or Head of Implementation with structured tiers (Tier-1, Tier-2, enterprise) and a dedicated support queue for implementation issues

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Customer Onboarding Lead: when the role owns the technical configuration sequence and go-live milestone gate, not just onboarding content delivery
- Solutions Consultant (post-sale scope): when the role transitions from pre-sale architecture to post-sale implementation ownership

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- Customer Success Manager / Account Manager: the Customer Advocate archetype — takes the handoff at go-live and owns the ongoing relationship, not the implementation itself
- CS Operations Manager / Director: the Customer Operator archetype — owns the health model and platform configuration, not the individual account delivery
- VP of Customer Success / CCO: the Customer Leader archetype — owns portfolio NRR and CS team strategy, not implementation execution

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily:** The Customer Technician tracks active implementation status across their book. Which accounts are in configuration, which cleared an integration milestone, which have a blocker the customer hasn't responded to. They send follow-up messages, push engineering for product-blocker resolution, and check whether yesterday's integration event landed correctly in the platform.

**Weekly:** Go-live velocity review across their active portfolio — which accounts are ahead of plan, which are behind and why. Integration milestone completion check: how many new accounts cleared the first integration within the first 10 days of kickoff. Stakeholder breadth audit: how many active implementations have three or more named stakeholders on the project plan. Any implementation that has been in configuration for more than two weeks without a milestone gets a recovery plan.

**Monthly:** Implementation NPS from recent go-lives — what did the customer experience look like by segment (mid-market, enterprise, Tier-1, Tier-2)? Handoff quality review: are the structured handoff briefs complete and acknowledged by the receiving CSM within 48 hours? Product-blocker resolution time: how long from flag to resolution on issues that require an Engineering fix?

**Quarterly:** Cohort analysis on TTFV — which cohorts reached first value fastest and what they had in common (pre-kickoff use-case capture completeness, stakeholder breadth, complexity tier). Template and playbook review: does the configuration template still match the product's current onboarding requirements? Vertical-segment performance review: which verticals run the fastest implementations and what practices drive the difference?

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. AEs who close deals without capturing use-case details.**
The Implementation Manager gets a kickoff call with a company that signed three days ago and has no documentation of what they actually bought the product to do. The discovery that should have happened in the sales cycle happens again in week one of the implementation. Go-live slips. The customer's first experience of the product is an interrogation about their own business. *Goal-pursuit friction: Time-to-Value and Onboarding Velocity — Goal 1.*

**2. Implementations that stall because only one person on the customer side shows up.**
Kickoff was attended by one coordinator who has no configuration authority. The technical lead hasn't been introduced. The executive sponsor hasn't been briefed. The Implementation Manager spends the first month trying to escalate past the single point of contact and get the right stakeholders into the room before the timeline expires. *Goal-pursuit friction: Stakeholder Activation and Adoption — Goal 2.*

**3. Product blockers that take two weeks to resolve.**
A data integration fails because of an undocumented API constraint. The Implementation Manager files a ticket. Engineering triages it in three days, resolves it in nine more. The implementation timeline has now slipped a week. Every product-blocker that takes longer than four or five days to resolve adds directly to the go-live cycle. *Goal-pursuit friction: Cross-functional Handoffs and Integration Quality — Goal 3.*

**4. Customers who skip the configuration steps that matter most.**
The onboarding checklist has 22 steps. Most customers complete 8 of them. The ones who skip the integration setup, the use-case configuration, and the admin role assignment are the same ones who call support in week three saying the product "isn't working." The Implementation Manager knows the checklist is the difference between a successful go-live and a repeat-onboarding conversation six weeks later. *Goal-pursuit friction: Stakeholder Activation and Adoption — Goal 2.*

**5. CSMs who receive an incomplete handoff and have to reverse-engineer what happened in the implementation.**
The handoff brief should have the integration status, the configuration decisions, the stakeholder map, the open items, and the implementation NPS score. What arrives is a Salesforce note from the AE and a Slack message that says "they're live." The CSM has to reconstruct the account history in the first QBR. *Goal-pursuit friction: Cross-functional Handoffs and Integration Quality — Goal 3.*

## 6. What would offend them

**Individual rep or CSM performance comparisons.** A card that shows which Implementation Manager has the slowest average go-live time by name is a manager conversation, not an intelligence story. Implementation velocity is surfaced at the cohort and segment level, not the individual level.

**Finance, GL, or compensation data.** The Customer Technician doesn't own contract value, renewal risk, or revenue recognition. They own the delivery cycle. GL-level data is a Customer Leader and Finance conversation.

**Health score judgments on accounts still in implementation.** An account that hasn't reached go-live doesn't have a meaningful health score yet. Cards that frame an in-implementation account as "at-risk" based on health model outputs that are premature treat a timing question as a risk signal.

**Team capacity or headcount recommendations.** How many Implementation Managers to hire and how to structure the PS team are Customer Leader and People decisions. The Customer Technician receives intelligence about delivery quality, not workforce planning inputs.

**Strategy recommendations about which accounts to prioritize.** Implementation sequence and priority is governed by contract commitments and resource capacity, not by card-level advice about where to focus first.

## 8. Signal shapes worth distinguishing

The Customer Technician's data produces signals that look related but answer different questions. Cards must respect that separation.

**P-CT-01 (48-hour integration cohort TTFV retention lift) is distinct from P-CT-03 (data integration milestone completion rate).** Both are integration signals, but they measure different things at different timeframes. P-CT-01 (2.3x 90-day retention) is a retention outcome correlated with early integration speed — it measures what happens months later when an account connects its first integration within 48 hours of kickoff. P-CT-03 (71% completing the integration milestone within 10 days) is a current-quarter execution rate — it measures the proportion of active implementations that cleared a specific milestone on schedule this quarter. One is a retention signal grounded in cohort history; the other is a current delivery quality indicator.

**P-CT-02 (portfolio go-live velocity) is distinct from P-CT-05 (configuration sign-off velocity).** Both measure speed, but at different stages of the implementation cycle. P-CT-02 (mid-market 6 days ahead of plan) is a full-cycle metric — the total kickoff-to-go-live cycle for a cohort of completed implementations versus the project plan baseline. P-CT-05 (configuration sign-off Day 11 versus Day 18) is a stage-specific metric — how fast the configuration review step resolved within enterprise implementations using the pre-kickoff template. One measures the total cycle; the other measures a specific stage within it.

**P-CT-04 (multi-stakeholder activation Day 30 retention) is distinct from P-CT-11 (stakeholder breadth at kickoff).** Both are stakeholder signals, but they measure different moments and outcomes. P-CT-11 (64% of kickoffs with 3+ named stakeholders) is an input metric measured at the start of implementation — it tells you how many implementations launched with the right stakeholder coverage in place. P-CT-04 (3+ users by Day 30 = 3x 90-day retention) is an output metric measured at the 30-day mark — it tells you what happens to retention when multi-user activation is achieved in the first month. One is a process quality indicator; the other is a retention outcome.

**P-CT-09 (product-blocker resolution velocity) is distinct from P-CT-13 (implementation support response velocity).** Both are resolution-speed signals, but they involve different teams and different problem types. P-CT-09 (9 days to 4 days) is the median time from a product-side technical blocker being flagged to Engineering resolution — it measures Engineering's responsiveness to implementation-specific issues. P-CT-13 (6 hours to 2.4 hours) is the median first-response time on implementation support tickets — it measures the support team's response to customer-raised questions and issues during active implementations. One is an Engineering output; the other is a support queue metric.

**P-CT-15 (self-serve sandbox launch and Tier-1 activation lift) is distinct from P-CT-08 (Tier-2 activation within 14 days).** Both are activation-rate signals, but they describe different complexity tiers and different drivers. P-CT-08 (Tier-2 implementations reaching first value inside 14 days at 78%) is a delivery execution rate for the mid-complexity tier — it reflects the Customer Technician team's throughput at a specific complexity segment this quarter. P-CT-15 (sandbox launch lifting Tier-1 from 62% to 81%) is a product-event-to-activation signal — a specific product launch (self-serve sandbox, March 12) changed the activation rate for the simplest complexity tier by enabling pre-kickoff exploration. One measures CS delivery execution; the other measures a product change's effect on the activation funnel.

## 9. Organizational Integration

**Upward (what the Customer Technician's work must visibly serve):**
The Customer Technician's outputs feed the Customer Leader's and Customer Operator's portfolio visibility. Go-live velocity, TTFV, and stakeholder activation rates flow upward as leading indicators of renewal health — accounts that reach first value faster retain better, and the Customer Leader tracks this correlation at the portfolio level. The Customer Operator receives onboarding completion milestones and integration quality signals as inputs to the health score model; a clean go-live with high activation breadth produces a stronger Day-0 health score than a prolonged, stakeholder-thin implementation.

**Downward (what the Customer Technician directs and is accountable for):**
At the IC level, the Customer Technician has no direct reports in the standard structure. Their accountability flows through the delivery project: every stakeholder who participates in kickoff, every integration milestone that lands on schedule, and every configuration sign-off that clears by the expected date reflects the Customer Technician's ability to run the implementation program.

**Horizontal (dependencies and handoffs):**
- Revenue Generator (AE): The Customer Technician's most critical upstream dependency. The quality of the use-case capture at close determines whether the implementation starts from a known position or has to reconstruct scope from scratch. Complete pre-close documentation shortens the implementation cycle by a measurable number of days.
- Customer Advocate (CSM): The Customer Technician's primary handoff target. The structured handoff brief at go-live is the formal transfer of implementation knowledge to the ongoing relationship owner. A complete brief — integration status, configuration decisions, stakeholder map, open items — is the difference between a CSM who inherits an account versus one who has to rebuild its history.
- Customer Operator: Shares the integration layer. The Customer Technician delivers the integration milestones that feed into the health model the Customer Operator maintains. Onboarding completion rates, activation milestones, and data integration events flow from implementation delivery into the Operator's model inputs.
- Product / Engineering: The Customer Technician depends on Engineering for product-blocker resolution. Technical issues that require a fix in the product — API constraints, configuration bugs, integration incompatibilities — sit in Engineering's queue. Resolution velocity on implementation-tagged blockers directly affects go-live cycle time.
