# Marketing Builder, Intelligence Brief

> Phase B, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Marketing Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Marketing Builder is the practitioner who executes the campaigns, manages the marketing stack, and owns the operational systems that translate strategy into demand. They run paid channels (search, social, display), produce content that earns organic pipeline, maintain the routing and attribution infrastructure that connects marketing activity to revenue, and build the sales enablement assets reps actually use. At mid-market B2B SaaS companies in 2026, this role carries titles like Demand Generation Manager, Marketing Manager, Content Marketing Manager, Marketing Operations Manager (MOps), Marketing Coordinator, or Growth Marketer.

They report into the Marketing Leader or a Director of Demand Gen. Some carry "Campaign" or "Growth" or "Digital" in their title. At smaller companies one person covers all four areas; at larger companies the archetype splits into specialists.

A good week is a webinar campaign that generates a third of April's MQLs in its first 48 hours, a paid CPL that held flat while the rest of the industry saw inflation, an SEO refresh that moves a priority keyword from page 2 to position 2 in nine days, and the battlecard adoption dashboard showing 38 of 47 reps actually used the asset. A difficult week is discovering that a new campaign's UTM parameters weren't stamped correctly, meaning three weeks of pipeline attribution is ambiguous, or that the inbound routing queue backed up overnight and 80 demos waited 40 minutes for first touch.

The Marketing Builder operates at channel, campaign, and asset level. Their scope is execution and optimization — not strategy, not messaging architecture, and not individual rep or customer management. Lens's job for this archetype is to surface the signal in operational and channel data that shows what's working, what changed, and where the system is tightening or loosening.

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Demand Generation Manager / Director of Demand Gen
- Marketing Operations Manager (MOps) / VP Marketing Operations
- Content Marketing Manager / Senior Content Manager / SEO Manager
- Growth Marketer / Performance Marketing Manager
- Digital Marketing Manager / Paid Media Manager
- Marketing Coordinator / Marketing Associate (execution-level)

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Revenue Operations Analyst where MOps is the primary accountability
- Campaign Manager at companies where one role owns paid + content + ops
- Marketing Automation Specialist where the primary output is campaign infrastructure

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- Marketing Leader (VP/CMO altitude): the Marketing Builder reports to this person
- Marketing Strategist (PMM / positioning altitude): sets the message the Marketing Builder executes
- Revenue Developer (SDR altitude): the speed-to-lead SLA the Marketing Builder maintains feeds this person's queue

**Seniority notes:** The Marketing Builder archetype begins when the person is the final accountability point for a channel or operational system — paid CPL, MQL volume, routing SLA, content pipeline, asset adoption. A Marketing Coordinator who executes campaigns designed by a Director is at the entry end; a Demand Gen Director who owns the full funnel from campaign to MQL is at the senior end. Both operate at execution altitude.

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily/Weekly:** Paid channel performance — CPL, ROAS, pipeline pacing against monthly target. Inbound queue management — routing SLA compliance, speed-to-lead metrics. Campaign launch and optimization — A/B test status, creative rotation, audience refresh. Marketing automation hygiene — form completion rates, UTM integrity, Marketo-to-Salesforce sync.

**Monthly:** Content performance review — organic traffic, keyword ranking movements, content-attributable pipeline. MQL quality audit — field completeness, source attribution, SQL conversion by source. Attribution reconciliation — cross-referencing Marketo and Salesforce sourcing data. Sales enablement asset usage — Highspot opens, battlecard utilization, calculator adoption.

**Quarterly:** Full channel attribution report. SEO priority keyword audit. Marketing tech stack review. Budget pacing vs. plan. Demand gen retrospective — which campaigns generated qualified pipeline, which generated volume without quality.

**Ad hoc:** Routing logic updates when inbound volume spikes or after-hours coverage gaps emerge. UTM cleanup sprints when attribution variance is flagged in CRM. Webinar logistics — registration page, email sequence, follow-up routing.

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. UTM integrity breaks and attribution falls apart.**
The Marketing Builder's attribution chain depends on every paid click, every form fill, and every email click carrying a properly stamped UTM parameter. One campaign without proper UTMs means three weeks of pipeline shows up as "direct" in Salesforce and the Builder can't prove the campaign drove it. *Goal-pursuit friction: attribution accuracy and pipeline credit.*

**2. Inbound routing logic fails at peak volume.**
When the webinar campaign generates 300 demo requests in 11 days, the routing queue can't handle after-hours volume, and demos wait 40 minutes instead of 4. The first-touch SLA breaks and SQL conversion drops. The routing logic is in their Marketo workflows, and updating it requires a change request that can take days. *Goal-pursuit friction: speed-to-lead and MQL quality.*

**3. Content produces traffic but pipeline attribution is contested.**
The pricing-page post ranks position 2, it gets 12,000 sessions, and the Marketing Builder can't cleanly connect those sessions to the four deals in the pipeline that touched the post. The attribution window debate between last-touch and multi-touch means the content team rarely gets full credit. *Goal-pursuit friction: content ROI and channel attribution.*

**4. Paid CPL is always climbing except when it's not, and explaining why is hard.**
If LinkedIn CPL holds flat across three weeks while the rest of the industry sees a 12% MoM increase, that's a win — but it takes a data pull, a benchmark comparison, and a slide to communicate it. If it spikes, it takes the same effort to diagnose whether it's audience fatigue, creative decay, or platform-level CPM inflation. *Goal-pursuit friction: paid efficiency and budget justification.*

**5. Sales doesn't use the assets the Marketing Builder builds.**
The ROI calculator took three weeks to build. If 22 of 36 mid-market AEs are actually using it in live deals, that's a meaningful adoption rate — but the Marketing Builder usually doesn't know until they pull the Highspot data manually. Without closed-loop visibility, they can't tell what's working or build the case for the next asset investment. *Goal-pursuit friction: sales enablement ROI and asset adoption.*

**6. Form field completeness degrades every time a new campaign goes live.**
Each new landing page introduces new form fields. Without a scoring threshold on what constitutes a complete MQL handoff, forms proliferate, field completion rates fall, and sales receives MQLs with missing UTM, channel, or persona data. *Goal-pursuit friction: MQL quality at handoff.*

## 6. What would offend them

**Treating attribution as settled.** The Marketing Builder has spent real time arguing over pipeline credit with RevOps, Finance, and Sales. Framing attribution as a clean solved problem signals they've never seen the inside of the Marketo-Salesforce sync report.

**Confusing demand generation with marketing strategy.** The Marketing Builder executes the strategy the Marketing Strategist writes. Collapsing the two — treating campaign execution as positioning work or vice versa — misses the scope of their actual accountability.

**Presenting CPL without context.** A $138 CPL means nothing without the prior period, the channel benchmark, and the audience context (did the audience refresh drive the change or did platform CPMs move). Decontextualized metrics are noise.

**Suggesting they "just fix the attribution."** UTM integrity is a continuous operational discipline, not a one-time fix. Treating it as a one-off task signals misunderstanding of the operational reality.

**Presenting individual rep or customer data.** The Marketing Builder works at channel, campaign, and asset level. Individual rep quotas, individual customer health scores, and individual deal progression are not their scope. Aggregate signals (how many reps opened the asset, what is the median speed-to-lead across all demos) are the right altitude.

## 8. Signal shapes worth distinguishing

**Paid pipeline pacing is distinct from paid CPL efficiency.** $1.18M of $1.5M pipeline target reached by week 3 is a volume signal — the channel is producing at pace. LinkedIn CPL holding flat at $142 while March CPL climbed 12% is an efficiency signal — the audience refresh is working. Both signals are worth surfacing independently because they answer different questions (are we going to hit the target vs. is our cost structure sustainable).

**Webinar MQL volume is distinct from webinar MQL quality.** 312 MQLs from the April 9 webinar in 11 days is a volume signal. A 19% SQL conversion rate on webinar-sourced MQLs versus 11% for paid social in the same window is a quality signal. Volume and quality can move independently: a campaign can generate high MQL volume from a low-quality audience, or a small campaign can produce fewer but better-converting MQLs. Surface both when the data supports both.

**Routing SLA compliance is distinct from speed-to-lead.** 95% of inbound demos routed inside the 5-minute SLA is a system-health signal — the routing logic is working. A 4.2-minute median speed-to-lead is an outcome signal — the median experience. The two are related but not identical: high SLA compliance can coexist with a skewed median if a small number of slow outliers drive the average up.

**Attribution variance rate is distinct from attribution coverage.** 2.1% Marketo-Salesforce mismatch rate on Q2 opportunities is an accuracy signal — the system is clean. 88% of April pipeline carrying a clean attribution chain is a completeness signal — how much of the pipeline the system covers. Both matter to the Marketing Builder because accuracy tells them whether the attribution they have is trustworthy, and coverage tells them whether there are gaps they're blind to.

**Organic traffic growth is distinct from keyword ranking.** 28% session growth on the comparison hub is a traffic signal — users are finding the pages. A priority keyword moving from position 7 to position 2 is a ranking signal — the content is winning on a specific query. Traffic can grow even when rankings are flat (due to a mix shift toward higher-volume queries), and rankings can improve without immediate traffic lift (if the query has low volume). Surface both when the data supports both.

## 9. Adjacent roles and interfaces

**Marketing Leader / CMO (upward):** The Marketing Builder's metrics feed the CMO's channel ROI narrative and pipeline contribution reporting. They execute against the budget and channel mix the Marketing Leader sets.

**Marketing Strategist / PMM (horizontal — highest-frequency peer):** The Marketing Builder executes campaigns against the positioning and messaging the Marketing Strategist defines. Misalignment is common: demand gen runs campaigns on last quarter's positioning while PMM has already moved to a new frame.

**Revenue Developer / SDR (horizontal — primary queue consumer):** The Marketing Builder's inbound routing SLA determines how fast SDRs receive demo requests. Speed-to-lead directly affects SQL conversion rates. The Marketing Builder owns the infrastructure; the SDR owns the first call.

**Revenue Operator / RevOps (horizontal — attribution partner):** The Marketing Builder's UTM infrastructure feeds the RevOps attribution model in Salesforce. Marketo-Salesforce sync quality is a joint accountability. Attribution disputes between Marketing and Sales live at this interface.

**Revenue Generator / AE (horizontal — enablement consumer):** AEs are the Marketing Builder's primary audience for battlecards, ROI calculators, and one-pagers. Highspot adoption data is the signal the Marketing Builder has on whether the asset is being used; deal outcomes are the signal AEs care about.
