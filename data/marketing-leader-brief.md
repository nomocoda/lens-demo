# Marketing Leader, Intelligence Brief

> Phase A, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Marketing Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Marketing Leader in B2B SaaS is the person accountable for two things that the organization treats as one thing and the market treats as separate: demand and belief. Their core job is generating qualified pipeline while simultaneously building a market position strong enough to make that pipeline easier and cheaper to close over time. A good day looks like attribution telling a coherent story, a campaign that just moved an enterprise account through the funnel, and a CEO who doesn't call at 4pm asking why pipeline is 20% light with six weeks left in the quarter. A bad day is when Sales rejects the latest MQL batch as unqualified, the SEO dashboard is red again, or someone in the board prep asks what marketing actually contributed to the last three closed-won deals.

This is the person who describes their job as "building the machine that makes revenue predictable", and who privately knows that machine is harder to build than anyone upstairs appreciates. They sit at the intersection of brand, demand, product narrative, and customer intelligence. They talk to customers more than any other leader outside of CS, because they know the difference between what the ICP says in a demo and what they say to a peer on a Pavilion Slack thread. They think in pipelines and funnels and win rates, but they also think in stories, because the two are the same thing at different zoom levels. They describe their role not as "marketing" but as "the function that makes growth predictable" (Gartner CMO Spend Survey, 2025; Exit Five community research, 2024-2025).

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Chief Marketing Officer (CMO)
- VP of Marketing
- VP of Growth (when the role owns demand generation and brand)
- Head of Marketing (50-150 employee range; often a VP equivalent without the title)

**Seniority variations:**
- 50-150 employees: Often one senior leader who is 70% execution, 30% strategy; may or may not have a team below them
- 150-350 employees: Typically a VP with 3-8 direct reports spanning demand gen, content, and product marketing
- 350-500 employees: Full CMO with a leadership team; may have a separate VP Demand Gen and VP Product Marketing reporting in

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Fractional CMO: Structurally external but operationally equivalent, attending weekly marketing-sales syncs, owning the attribution model, presenting to the board
- VP Brand and Marketing: Fits when the role owns demand generation alongside brand, not purely a brand-only function

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- Director of Marketing: One level below; owns execution channels without owning pipeline accountability or board-level reporting
- VP Product Marketing: Fits only when the PMM leader also owns pipeline attribution; if scoped to enablement and messaging only, this is the Marketing Strategist archetype
- VP Demand Generation: Fits when demand gen owns the full marketing P&L and pipeline number, but not when demand gen is a sub-function reporting to a Marketing Leader
- CMO of a 5,000-person enterprise: Different altitude, operating context diverges too far from 50-500 employee B2B SaaS

*Sources: Kalungi fractional CMO guide (2025); PipelineRoad B2B SaaS CMO guide (2025); CMO Alliance CMO Insights Report (2025, n=119 CMOs); Pavilion CMO community composition*

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily:** Marketing Leaders start their morning with the dashboard, pipeline coverage trends, lead volume versus target, channel performance, any anomalies in the MQL-to-SQL conversion rate. They check if anything broke overnight: a paid campaign gone dark, a landing page conversion that fell off a cliff, a spike in unsubscribes. This takes 30-60 minutes and sets the agenda. Team standups and async Slack are woven through the rest of the day.

**Weekly:** The weekly rhythm centers on the marketing-sales pipeline review, the meeting where both sides look at the same numbers and usually disagree about what they mean. Marketing Leaders also run a channel performance review internally (MQL volume, CPL by source, pipeline generated, pipeline influenced), one-on-ones with each functional lead, and a budget pacing check versus the quarter's plan. They spend 4-5 hours on strategic work: positioning refinement, competitive intelligence, messaging workshops, board prep. Kalungi fractional CMO data (2025) reports: CEO reporting = 1h/week, dashboard management = 1h/week, team one-on-ones = 6h/week, vendor/agency management = 3h/week, strategic work = 4h/week.

**Monthly:** End-of-month is attribution audit time, which channels sourced and influenced pipeline, what CAC by acquisition cohort looks like, whether the marketing efficiency ratio is moving in the right direction. Content calendar review, ICP accuracy check versus recent closed-won and closed-lost data, and a deep-dive with RevOps on attribution model health. Competitive landscape refresh, typically a 30-60 minute read through Klue or Crayon alerts plus any noteworthy G2 category movement.

**Quarterly:** Budget defense and reallocation. Marketing QBR with agency partners. OKR review and reset. Positioning refresh if deal-outcome data has signaled ICP drift. Some Marketing Leaders run a formal deal-outcome interview cycle quarterly, internally or through a third party, specifically to hear from the market in unfiltered form.

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. Attribution is broken, and it's going to cost me my budget.**
The fundamental measurement problem: 90% of B2B marketing teams face attribution challenges due to fragmented data and siloed systems (RevSure, cited in Heinz Marketing Mid-Market Challenges, 2025). Only 36% of leaders who prioritize demonstrating ROI can actually measure it accurately. Without proper attribution, 30-40% of budget goes to channels that can't prove proportional returns. For the Marketing Leader, this is existential, the attribution model is the story they tell the CFO. When it's wrong, the story falls apart at the worst moment. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 1 (Measurable Growth and ROI).*

**2. Sales says our leads are garbage. But nobody can define what a good lead looks like.**
The MQL definition disagreement surfaces in nearly every team's marketing-sales review. Only 27% of marketing's leads convert to sales-accepted leads in most organizations, but this number is artificially depressed by misaligned qualification criteria, not necessarily poor lead quality (Pipeline360, 2025; Landbase alignment research, 2026). The friction rarely gets resolved because neither team owns the SLA together. Marketing Leaders feel this as a political problem masquerading as a data problem. *Cross-functional friction: Marketing ↔ Revenue Generator / Revenue Operator.*

**3. Budgets are flat. Expectations aren't.**
59% of CMOs say they lack sufficient budget to execute their strategy (Gartner CMO Spend Survey, 2025). Budgets have flatlined at 7.7% of company revenue; 63% of senior marketing leaders in a September 2025 Gartner survey cited budget constraints as their #1 challenge. Meanwhile, CEO pipeline expectations increase each quarter. Marketing Leaders absorb this tension through creative reallocation, but over time it degrades channel experimentation and brand investment. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 1 (Measurable Growth and ROI).*

**4. The organic traffic our blog sent to the funnel is gone, and we haven't replaced it yet.**
73% of B2B websites experienced significant traffic shrinkage between 2024 and 2025, with an average 34% YoY decline (Digital Bloom 2025 Organic Traffic Crisis Report). 60% of all Google searches now end without a click to any website. AI Overviews reduced CTR from position one from approximately 28% to 8%. HubSpot's CEO acknowledged publicly: "organic search traffic is declining globally" and AI overviews are "giving answers." The content investment that built many SaaS companies' inbound pipelines is harder to defend now. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 1 (Measurable Growth and ROI) and Goal 2 (Brand and Value Proposition).*

**5. Nobody believes brand has ROI until we lose a deal to 'never heard of you.'**
Half of B2B SaaS companies don't track brand health, and only a quarter measure it regularly. The Marketing Leader knows that brand credibility shortens sales cycles, raises win rates, and strengthens pricing power, but they're in a perennial budget argument about it. When the proof arrives, it arrives as a deal lost: a competitive deal where the finalist's brand recognition gave them the edge. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 2 (Brand and Value Proposition).*

**6. Our ICP was right 18 months ago. The product has moved, the market has moved, our messaging hasn't.**
ICP drift is a constant low-grade pain: economic shifts, new buyer personas, competitive moves, and product expansion all cause the ideal customer profile to evolve faster than most teams update their positioning (ICP Drift Guide, 2025; cojoyrevgen.com ICP drift research). Marketing Leaders at 50-500 employee companies often discover the drift in a deal-outcome review, not proactively. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 3 (Customer Centricity).*

## 6. What would offend them

**"Marketing is the creative team."** Treating this function as a creative execution arm while RevOps and Finance own all pipeline analytics is the fastest way to lose a Marketing Leader's trust. They're accountable for pipeline. They expect access to and ownership of attribution infrastructure, funnel conversion data, and spend-efficiency metrics. Reducing the function to campaign creative signals the speaker doesn't understand what modern B2B marketing leadership actually does.

**Last-click attribution presented as "marketing ROI."** Marketing Leaders know that the first two-thirds of the B2B buying journey is invisible to last-click models. An interface that reports last-click attribution as the primary measure of marketing's contribution will be dismissed immediately. Dave Gerhardt's Exit Five attribution episode (ep. 239, 2025) explicitly frames last-click as "dead", this is a widely shared practitioner view. The comment in that episode: "Just because an event happened right after another event does not make the first event the cause."

**Activity metrics presented as outcomes.** Emails sent, posts published, webinar registrations, blog traffic, all inputs, not outputs. A dashboard that surfaces these as primary indicators of marketing health signals the tool doesn't understand what the role is being measured on. The Marketing Leader wants pipeline generated, pipeline influenced, CAC by cohort, MQL-to-SQL conversion, win rate by channel. Everything else is supporting context.

**Calling marketing a cost center in any reporting frame.** 78% of SaaS CMOs now have direct revenue targets (CMO Alliance, 2025). Framing the function as a cost center is a structural misunderstanding with real budget consequences. Marketing Leaders have been working to shift this framing for years and are acutely sensitive to it.

**"Demand gen" used interchangeably with "lead gen."** These are different things. Lead gen is volume. Demand gen is cultivating genuine market interest, it includes the 95% of the market not in an active buying cycle but who will be. Conflating them signals unfamiliarity with how senior marketing practitioners think about pipeline creation versus market development. (Exit Five / practitioner community consensus; Forrester 2026 B2B buyer research.)

**Individual SDR performance data surfaced to the Marketing Leader.** This role cares about pipeline coverage, channel conversion, and ICP match rate, business-cut aggregates. Individual rep data belongs in a sales performance review, not a marketing intelligence interface. Mixing the two conflates two different accountability structures.

## 8. Adjacent roles and interfaces

**Revenue Leader / CRO:** The Marketing Leader's most important peer relationship and highest-potential friction point. Pipeline generation is the shared metric, and the shared disagreement. Marketing owns sourced pipeline; the CRO owns closed revenue. The friction zone is in the handoff: MQL definition, lead routing, response time SLAs, and what happens to pipeline that sales doesn't touch. When pipeline misses, the CRO looks at marketing contribution first. This relationship requires a formal SLA and a shared attribution model to function. (Pipeline360, 2025; Landbase alignment research, 2026.)

**Revenue Generator (Account Executive / RSM):** The frontline of lead quality feedback. AEs experience marketing's pipeline as individual opportunities, they're the first to call out a bad MQL and the last to credit marketing for an assist. The Marketing Leader needs their qualitative signal (why are deals losing? what objections are coming up in discovery?) but gets it filtered through their manager or RevOps. This distance is a structural problem for ICP feedback loops.

**Revenue Operator (RevOps):** The Marketing Leader's infrastructure partner. RevOps controls the CRM configuration, attribution field setup, lead routing rules, and the data contracts that determine whether marketing's story about pipeline is credible. CRM fields have more to tell when complete, and the Marketing Leader depends on the Revenue Operator's system design to make that completeness possible. If attribution fields aren't set up correctly, the Marketing Leader can't prove their contribution. This is a high-trust, high-dependency relationship.

**Product / CPO:** Launch coordination and messaging alignment. Every major product release requires a joint GTM motion: marketing owns the narrative, product owns the roadmap. Friction peaks when product ships without enough marketing runway to build demand, or when marketing positions a feature differently than product intended. The Marketing Leader is usually reconciling these two versions of the story in front of customers. (Forrester 2026; PMA State of Product Marketing 2025.)

**Finance / CFO:** Budget defense is the primary interface. The Marketing Leader must translate campaign investment into financial outcomes, CAC payback, marketing efficiency ratio, pipeline coverage, in a language that maps to how Finance models the business. 59% of CMOs cite insufficient budget as their top challenge (Gartner, 2025); most of those conversations happen with a CFO who applies a different discount rate to brand investment than to performance marketing.

**Customer Success / VP CS:** The Marketing Leader needs customers, for case studies, reference calls, advocacy, and renewal expansion signals. CS owns those relationships. Friction appears when marketing wants to activate a customer for a case study and CS says the account is in a sensitive renewal conversation. The expansion and advocacy signals CS sees are also among the richest ICP signals marketing can use to sharpen targeting.

**CEO:** The direct report relationship. The CEO holds the Marketing Leader accountable for two things simultaneously: hitting the pipeline number this quarter, and building a brand and category position that makes next year's number easier. These accountability streams pull in opposite directions when budgets are tight, short-term demand gen versus long-term brand investment. The Marketing Leader spends significant energy managing this tension in both directions.
