# Revenue Leader, Intelligence Brief

> Phase A, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Revenue Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Revenue Leader in B2B SaaS is the person who carries the number for the company. Their accountability is binary in a way most leadership roles are not: did the team hit the commit, and does the forecast they're committing upward this quarter still hold next quarter. A good day looks like coverage strong enough that the forecast feels boring, three large deals moving on the timeline they promised, and a Friday pipeline review where every AE's commit holds up to a stage-history audit. A bad day is a stage-skipped deal slipping out of the quarter, a coverage chart that just dropped under 3x, or a CFO email asking why ARR pacing is 8 points behind plan.

This is the person who describes their job as "making the number predictable" and who privately understands that most of the variance in a quarter comes from a handful of deals at the top of the funnel and a handful of decisions at the bottom. They sit at the intersection of pipeline coverage, deal execution, win/loss patterns, and competitive dynamics. They live in the forecast spreadsheet, the deal review agenda, and the win/loss readouts. They think in pipeline coverage ratios, stage-conversion rates, and weighted forecasts, but they also think in narratives, because the CEO and the board want a story that explains the math. They describe their role not as "sales" but as "the function that turns pipeline into committed revenue."

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Chief Revenue Officer (CRO)
- VP of Sales
- Sales Director (in 50-150 employee orgs where there's no VP layer)
- Head of Sales (50-150 employee range; often a VP equivalent without the title)
- Regional VP (in larger orgs with multiple Revenue Leaders by geography)

**Seniority variations:**
- 50-150 employees: Often a player-coach VP of Sales who closes top deals while managing a small team
- 150-350 employees: Typically a VP of Sales with 4-10 AE reports plus an SDR manager and a sales-ops partner
- 350-500 employees: CRO with multiple VPs reporting in (VP Sales, VP Customer Success, VP Sales Development)

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Fractional CRO during a transition period: structurally external but operationally equivalent
- VP Revenue: when the role owns both new business and post-sale expansion

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- AE / Account Executive: the Revenue Generator archetype
- SDR Manager / VP Sales Development: the Revenue Developer archetype
- VP RevOps / Sales Ops Director: the Revenue Operator archetype
- VP Customer Success: the Customer Leader archetype, distinct accountability

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily:** Revenue Leaders open the forecast first. They check what moved overnight: stage changes, close-date moves, new opps in the >$100K threshold, stalled deals. They walk through the day's deal reviews, sit in on at least one customer meeting, and close the day with a forecast roll-up to the CEO if it's near quarter-end.

**Weekly:** The weekly rhythm centers on pipeline review with the AE team and forecast call with the executive team. They pressure-test every commit-stage deal: stage progression, last contact, decision criteria, procurement timing, competitive context. They also run win/loss review (deals closed in the week, why won, why lost), competitive intelligence updates from AEs, and a marketing-sourced pipeline read.

**Monthly:** End-of-month commit verification with Finance, ARR pacing review, segment-level performance audit (enterprise vs mid-market vs SMB), and a deeper competitive analysis on head-to-head deal patterns. Renewal forecast review with Customer Success.

**Quarterly:** Quarter-close intensity (last two weeks: every commit deal reviewed daily). Then the next quarter's coverage build, capacity planning with RevOps, territory rebalancing if performance asymmetries warrant it, and the QBR where the leader presents a full-year-to-date and forward-looking story to the board.

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. Forecasts move every week and I can't tell why.**
Stage progression that looks clean on a snapshot turns into a different story when you watch the close-date history. When too many deals slip a quarter, the issue is rarely sandbagging; it's that the AEs and the deal-stage definitions are no longer in sync with what's actually happening in the room. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 1 (Quarter Attainment and Forecast Reliability).*

**2. Coverage looks fine until you ask which deals.**
A 4x pipeline coverage ratio that's concentrated in five deals is structurally different from a 4x coverage ratio spread across thirty. The Revenue Leader knows the difference; the dashboards usually don't surface it. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 2 (Pipeline Coverage and Health).*

**3. Marketing's pipeline is bigger than ever, and our win rate hasn't moved.**
The Revenue Leader is counting on marketing-sourced pipeline to convert at a healthy rate. When sourced volume rises and win rate doesn't, something in the qualification chain has broken. *Cross-functional friction: Marketing ↔ Revenue.*

**4. We're winning less head-to-head than we used to.**
A 50/50 split versus a top competitor at the start of the year, and a 2-3 record by quarter-end, is a positioning signal not a sales-execution signal. The Revenue Leader needs the head-to-head pattern surfaced fast enough to inform messaging and demo content. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 3 (Win Rate and Competitive Position).*

**5. Renewals are landing at the wrong number.**
A renewal that comes in at 105% NRR when the segment averaged 112% last quarter is a signal hidden in plain sight. The Revenue Leader sees this in the aggregate; CS sees the account-level reasons. The handoff is where the friction lives. *Cross-functional friction: Revenue ↔ Customer Success.*

## 6. What would offend them

**Individual rep performance surfaced as if it were a quarter-pacing signal.** This role thinks in segment, source, channel, competitive cut, deal-size band. Rep-level performance is a coaching conversation, not a forecast conversation. Mixing the two is a category mistake.

**Activity metrics presented as health.** Calls made, emails sent, meetings booked, all inputs. The Revenue Leader is measured on closed-won ARR and committed-pipeline accuracy. A dashboard that surfaces activity as a primary signal misunderstands the role.

**Last-touch attribution offered as ICP feedback.** The deals that closed got there through a sequence of touches; pinning win/loss on the most recent one is a Marketing-side argument the Revenue Leader has heard too many times. Patterns matter; single-touch causality doesn't.

**A coverage ratio framed as "good" or "bad" against an external benchmark.** Coverage is contextual: enterprise pipeline at 4x in a long-cycle segment is healthy; mid-market pipeline at 4x in a fast-cycle segment is light. Grading is the leader's job, not the dashboard's.

**Forecast accuracy metrics that include deals the team didn't actually commit to.** The forecast is what the team committed; missed deals that weren't on the commit-line aren't forecast errors, they're stretch outcomes. Conflating the two erodes trust in the metric.

## 7. Five "that's me" quotes they would say

**Quote 1:** "Pipeline coverage looks fine on paper. Show me the deals."

**Quote 2:** "I don't trust the forecast until I've sat through the deal reviews myself."

**Quote 3:** "We're getting more leads, and the win rate hasn't moved. Something's off."

**Quote 4:** "Q3 is going to look ugly unless coverage builds in the next two weeks."

**Quote 5:** "If we keep losing head-to-head against Beacon, that's not a sales problem. That's a positioning problem."

## 8. Adjacent roles and interfaces

**CEO:** Direct report. The Revenue Leader carries the number to the CEO weekly and to the board quarterly. The CEO's tolerance for forecast variance is low; for win-rate variance, lower.

**Marketing Leader / CMO:** Primary peer relationship. Pipeline volume comes from Marketing; conversion is the Revenue Leader's. Friction sits at the SQL definition, response-time SLA, and source-attribution model.

**Revenue Generator (AEs):** The frontline of execution. Revenue Leaders coach the patterns: stage discipline, MEDDIC adoption, multithreading. They do not micro-manage individual deals; they read aggregate behavior.

**Revenue Operator (RevOps):** Infrastructure partner. RevOps owns the forecast model, stage definitions, CRM hygiene, and reporting. The Revenue Leader depends on RevOps to keep the data clean enough to trust.

**Customer Success / VP CS:** Renewal forecasts and expansion pipeline both flow through this relationship. NRR by segment is a shared metric; expansion-sourced ACV is a shared opportunity.

**Finance / CFO:** Budget defense for headcount, comp plan accuracy, ARR pacing. Conversations are quarterly and tense at quarter-close.

**Product / CPO:** Custom feature requests from late-stage deals are the friction point. The Revenue Leader needs roadmap commitments in the window the deal needs them; Product needs realistic commitment patterns.
