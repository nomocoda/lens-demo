# Revenue Developer, Intelligence Brief

> Phase A, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Revenue Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Revenue Developer in B2B SaaS is the pipeline creation specialist, the SDR or BDR who owns the top of the funnel. Their accountability is measured in meeting volume, AE-accepted opportunities, and the quality of what they hand off to quota-carrying account executives. They are the first human contact most prospects ever have with the company. A good day is a high-intent account picking up on the first touch, a subject-line test that doubles the reply rate, and four AE-accepted meetings booked. A bad day is a sequence burning through 50 contacts with a 1% reply rate, a batch of inbound leads sitting unworked past the speed-to-lead window, and a Friday afternoon with no meetings on the calendar.

This is the person who optimizes obsessively: which subject lines open, which first lines earn replies, which time of day the phone connects, which trigger events produce outsized response. They track their own funnel like a small experiment, always testing, always measuring, always looking for the one variable that moved the number. They live in the sequence tool, the dialer, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, and intent platforms. They need intelligence that tells them where the signal is strongest today and which outreach motion is producing results without being asked.

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Sales Development Representative (SDR)
- Business Development Representative (BDR)
- Market Development Representative (MDR)
- Inbound SDR (focused on demo-request routing and speed-to-lead)
- Outbound BDR (focused on cold outreach, sequence execution, and trigger-event response)

**Seniority variations:**
- 50-150 employees: Often a generalist SDR who handles both inbound routing and outbound sequencing
- 150-350 employees: Typically specialized into inbound SDR (speed-to-lead, demo routing) and outbound BDR (cold sequences, trigger events)
- 350-500 employees: May be segmented by territory, vertical, or target segment (SMB vs MM vs Enterprise BDR)

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Sales Associate: when the role handles qualification and meeting booking before handing to an AE
- Growth SDR: when the role owns pipeline expansion into new segments or verticals as a primary motion

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- AE / Account Executive: the Revenue Generator archetype, owns deal closure, not pipeline creation
- CRO / VP Sales: the Revenue Leader archetype, operates at segment and team level, not pipeline-creation level
- RevOps / Sales Ops: the Revenue Operator archetype, owns system and data quality, not outreach motions
- VP Customer Success: the Customer Leader archetype, owns retention and expansion, not pipeline

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily:** Revenue Developers open the sequence tool first. They check what replied overnight, what bounced, what opened without replying. They process inbound demo requests within the speed-to-lead window, route them to the right AE, and book the discovery call. They run their daily outbound block, dial the call list by time-of-day logic, and log every touch in the CRM before the session closes.

**Weekly:** The weekly rhythm centers on sequence performance review: which sequences are producing replies, which have gone cold, which have hit diminishing returns and need retirement. They review which accounts are crossing intent thresholds in 6sense or similar tools and shuffle them to the top of the outreach queue. They check the AE-accepted rate on the meetings they booked, the single most important quality signal they get back from the AE team.

**Monthly:** Sequence library refresh (retire underperformers, launch new tests), vertical or segment penetration review (which segments are responding faster), trigger-event list refresh (funding rounds, leadership changes, technology signals), and a look at touch-step distribution to confirm the multi-touch cadence is reaching prospects at the right depth.

**Quarterly:** Coverage planning for the next quarter, which segments or territories are underserved, which channels are producing efficiently, and what new test motions to run. AE-accepted rate review and tuning the definition of a qualified meeting with the revenue leader.

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. Inbound leads that age past the speed-to-lead window.**
A demo request lands on Friday afternoon. By Monday morning the prospect has received a competitor's reply. The SDR's job is to prevent that delay, which means weekend coverage and a system that catches leads before they go cold. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 1 (Pipeline Creation and Inbound Response).*

**2. Sequences that plateau after a few touches.**
The first two touches produce replies. Then the rate drops to near zero and stays there. The SDR does not know whether to extend the sequence, change the messaging, or retire the cadence and start fresh. Knowing where replies actually land in the sequence, touch 3, touch 7, or touch 10, changes the decision completely. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 2 (Sequence and Outreach Effectiveness).*

**3. AE rejection feedback arrives late or not at all.**
The SDR books 30 meetings a month. Ten get rejected by the AE. The SDR does not know whether the ICP targeting was off, the qualification questions were missed, or the segment itself isn't responding. The accepted rate is the quality signal; without it surfaced clearly, the SDR cannot tune. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 4 (AE Handoff Quality).*

**4. Intent signals that expire before outreach reaches them.**
A target account crosses a high-intent threshold on Monday. By Wednesday the SDR's outbound rotation has not reached them yet. The prospect who was in research mode has moved on. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 3 (ICP Targeting and Segment Penetration).*

**5. Trigger-event lists that are never refreshed.**
The SDR knows that reaching out within seven days of a Series B is 4x more effective than cold outreach. But the list of recently funded accounts needs to be refreshed weekly or the timing advantage disappears. When the list is fresh, the conversion is there. When it is stale, it is just another cold sequence. *Goal-pursuit friction: Goal 3 (ICP Targeting and Segment Penetration).*

## 6. What would offend them

**Individual rep performance surfaced as a scorecard.** The Revenue Developer tracks their own number obsessively. A card that surfaces their personal activity count or meeting volume relative to peers is a manager conversation, not an intelligence conversation. Signals are about patterns and motions, not about individual performance relative to other reps.

**Activity counts offered as outcomes.** Calls made, emails sent, LinkedIn messages sent, the Revenue Developer tracks these as inputs. The intelligence is whether those inputs produced replies, meetings, and AE-accepted handoffs. Surfacing inputs without the output signal is noise.

**Meeting volume without quality context.** Thirty meetings booked is a data point. Whether the AE accepted 84% or 62% of them tells the story. Volume without quality degrades the signal.

**Recommendations about which accounts to call.** The Revenue Developer manages their own territory. Telling them to "call Company X" or "re-sequence Account Y" is overreach. Lens surfaces where the pattern is strongest; the human decides the motion.

**Sequence effectiveness without the touch-step context.** A 4% reply rate on a sequence does not tell the whole story. Whether those replies land at touch 2 or touch 8 determines whether the sequence should be longer, shorter, or restructured. The distribution is the intelligence.

## 7. Five "that's me" quotes they would say

**Quote 1:** "I need to know which of my sequences is still producing and which one burned out three weeks ago. The aggregate reply rate hides it."

**Quote 2:** "We finally figured out the right time to call, 8 to 9 AM before everyone goes heads-down. Our connect rate tripled. That data should have been obvious a month ago."

**Quote 3:** "Healthcare is converting at a completely different rate than the rest of my book. I want to shift sequence coverage into that segment before someone else finds it."

**Quote 4:** "The subject-line test from Monday is already at 4.7%. I would have run that test six months ago if I had known the old opener was at 1.9%."

**Quote 5:** "Six accounts that went quiet 90 days ago replied this week. The new re-engagement sequence works. I need to scale it before the moment passes."

## 8. Signal shapes worth distinguishing

The Revenue Developer's mental model separates signals that look superficially similar but answer different questions. Cards must respect that separation. Conflating them collapses two cards into one and drops the more diagnostic of the two.

**Inbound speed-to-lead within the daily window is distinct from weekend-inbound catch-up coverage.** A signal about 17 of 18 leads reached within five minutes is a real-time routing signal, it describes the standard operating rhythm during working hours. A signal about all weekend demo requests reached by 9:15 Monday is a coverage completeness signal, it describes whether the team closes the off-hours window. Both are speed-to-lead, but the dynamics and the fixes are different.

**Multi-channel meeting rate versus email-only is distinct from where in the sequence replies land.** A 2.3x lift from multi-channel sequencing versus email-only compares channels as a strategy choice. Where in the sequence the breakthrough touch lands, touch 7 versus touch 4, is a sequencing depth signal that tells you whether to extend or shorten the cadence. Both touch multi-channel sequencing, but they answer different optimization questions.

**Subject-line variant lift and personalized first-line lift are distinct signals.** A subject-line variant test produces a lift in open-to-reply rate attributable to the line that gets the email opened. A personalized first-line test produces a lift in engagement once the email is opened. Both are copy tests, but the mechanism and the fix point differ, subject line optimization versus first-line personalization are not the same lever.

**Intent-platform account first-touch response and trigger-event list response are distinct signals.** An account crossing a 6sense high-intent threshold and responding on the first touch is a platform-signal confirmation story. An account within seven days of a Series B announcement responding to outreach is a trigger-event timing story. Both are signal-driven outreach, but the source of the intelligence is different, one is behavioral intent, one is firmographic event.

## 9. Adjacent roles and interfaces

**Revenue Leader (Manager):** The Revenue Developer's direct manager at companies with SDR team structures. The Revenue Leader reviews meeting volume, AE-accepted rate, and segment coverage weekly. The Revenue Developer's pipeline creation feeds the leader's aggregate coverage view.

**Revenue Generator (AE):** Pipeline handoff partner. The SDR books the meeting; the AE accepts or rejects it. The AE-accepted rate is the primary quality feedback loop from the AE team back to the SDR. Speed-to-first-call after acceptance is the secondary signal.

**Marketing Strategist:** Persona and messaging partner. Battlecard and positioning research from the Marketing Strategist informs the SDR's cold outreach messaging. Sequence performance data on which messages resonate flows back to the Strategist as signal. Launch campaigns create outbound triggers the SDR can use.

**Marketing Builder (Demand Gen):** Inbound routing partner. When a Marketing Builder's campaign produces a demo-request surge, the Revenue Developer is the person who processes those leads. Speed-to-lead on inbound is the shared metric, what the Marketing Builder produces, the Revenue Developer converts.

**Revenue Operator (RevOps):** Sequence tooling and routing partner. The Revenue Developer depends on RevOps for sequence tool uptime, routing rule accuracy, intent platform integrations, and lead assignment logic. When routing breaks or sequences do not load, the Revenue Developer loses prospecting time.

**Customer Advocate (CSM):** Re-engagement signal source. Churned or dormant accounts that the CSM deprioritizes may surface as re-engagement candidates for the Revenue Developer's outbound motion. Exit interview themes from the Customer Advocate provide the narrative hooks for re-engagement sequences.
