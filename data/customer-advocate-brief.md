# Customer Advocate, Intelligence Brief

> Phase A, Archetype Intelligence Brief | Customers Domain | B2B SaaS, 50-500 employees

## 1. Who this archetype actually is

The Customer Advocate is the CSM, the person who owns the relationship between Atlas SaaS and a named book of accounts. They are accountable for renewal readiness, expansion identification, and making sure every account they carry is seeing enough value to stay. Where the Customer Leader owns the portfolio number, the Customer Advocate owns the individual accounts that make up that number.

Their world is measured in health scores, QBR completion rates, stakeholder maps, and the quality of the relationships they have maintained. A good week is a QBR that surfaces new stakeholders, an early-renewal conversation that a green account welcomed, and a usage spike that converted into an expansion track. A bad week is discovering that the champion at a key account went quiet two months ago and nobody caught it, or a renewal conversation that surfaces budget pressure that had been building silently in the background.

The Customer Advocate lives at account-portfolio altitude, not company-wide altitude. Their intelligence frame is: across my 40-50 accounts, where is relationship depth growing, where is renewal momentum building, and which accounts are showing the usage patterns that precede expansion? They do not own the NRR forecast at the organizational level, that is the Customer Leader. They are accountable for the renewal and expansion signals that flow up to that forecast from their named accounts.

At mid-market B2B SaaS, the CSM carries 40-60 accounts depending on segment mix and company size. They are the human interface between the customer and every post-sale function: implementation, support, product, and executive escalation. The practitioner community describes them as carrying a "portfolio mindset at a relationship pace", they must think like a portfolio manager about risk distribution and opportunity concentration while showing up to every customer as though that account is the only one that matters.

## 2. Real roles that map to this archetype

**Core titles:**
- Customer Success Manager (CSM)
- Senior Customer Success Manager
- Account Manager (post-sale, where Account Management owns renewal and expansion)
- Customer Success Specialist (IC role, same accountability structure as CSM at smaller companies)
- Strategic Account CSM (named accounts with higher ARR thresholds)

**Edge cases that fit:**
- Enterprise CSM: same accountability frame but carrying 10-20 accounts at higher ACV rather than 40-60 mid-market accounts. The intelligence profile is identical; the portfolio depth changes.
- Named Account Manager: at companies where "Account Manager" is the CSM title, not a pre-sale role
- Technical Account Manager (TAM): when the TAM owns renewal readiness, expansion identification, and executive relationship depth, not just technical scoping

**Titles that sound similar but don't fit:**
- VP of Customer Success / CCO: the Customer Leader archetype, owns the NRR forecast and the portfolio-level strategy
- Director of CS Operations / CS Ops Manager: the Customer Operator archetype, owns systems, tooling, and process infrastructure
- Onboarding Specialist / Implementation Manager: the Customer Technician archetype, owns technical handoff and time-to-value, not ongoing relationship management
- Support Engineer / Technical Support: reactive support altitude, ticket-resolution frame, not proactive relationship and renewal management
- Account Executive (pre-sale AE): the Revenue Generator archetype, owns new-logo close, not post-sale renewal

**Seniority notes:** The Customer Advocate archetype begins when an IC carries a named book of accounts and is accountable for both renewal outcomes and expansion identification from those accounts. An SDR assigned to CS outreach campaigns is not a Customer Advocate. A CSM with a named book of 40 accounts who runs QBRs, tracks renewal pipeline, and surfaces expansion signals to AEs is the canonical Customer Advocate.

## 3. What they spend their time on

**Daily:** The Customer Advocate checks their book every morning. Which accounts have upcoming activity: renewals in the next 30-60-90 days, QBRs scheduled, champion contacts who have not responded to recent outreach. They log interactions, update CRM records (stakeholder maps, last-contact dates, health score notes), and triage any usage alerts or support escalations that touched their accounts overnight.

**Weekly:** Account review rhythm, which accounts are on track, which changed status since last week. Champion engagement check: how many accounts have a confirmed champion touchpoint logged in the last two weeks. Renewal pipeline review for upcoming cohort. Expansion signal check: which accounts crossed a usage or adoption threshold that should trigger an expansion conversation. Handoff review: did any new closed-won deals from the AE come across this week, and is the handoff package complete enough to start onboarding cleanly?

**Monthly:** QBR prep and execution for the strategic-tier book. Value story capture, ensuring every QBR produces a written value summary logged in CRM. Early-renewal conversations for accounts 90+ days from renewal that are green. Expansion pipeline development: converting usage spike signals into an AE-sourced expansion track. Book health review: distribution of accounts by segment, health color, and renewal window.

**Quarterly:** Cohort analysis with the Customer Leader: how did the renewal cohort perform versus commitment? Advocate pipeline cultivation: which satisfied accounts are candidates for case studies, reference calls, or marketing asks? CRM handoff quality audit: did Q's closed-won deals arrive with the stakeholder maps, buying reasons, and verbal commitments needed to onboard well?

## 5. What they complain about (and where it hurts)

**1. The champion who went dark before anyone noticed.**
The CSM's deepest fear is silent champion departure. The contact who was the day-to-day champion gradually stopped responding. The CRM showed the company as green because the aggregate health score didn't register a missing interaction. By the time the CSM noticed, the renewal conversation was already uncomfortable. The practitioner shorthand for this is "ghost churn", the account doesn't cancel loudly, it just goes quiet until the renewal date. *Goal-pursuit friction: Account Health and Relationship Depth, the foundational goal cluster.*

**2. QBRs that happen but don't matter.**
A QBR that is scheduled, runs on time, and produces no documented value story is a checkbox, not a touchpoint. The CSM knows whether the account left the QBR with a shared understanding of value or just watched a slide deck. When QBR completion rates are tracked but value-story capture rates are not, the metric looks good and the intelligence is missing. *Goal-pursuit friction: Value Delivery and QBR, Goal 4.*

**3. Expansion signals that expire before anyone acts on them.**
A usage spike on three accounts in the CSM's book is actionable intelligence today and history next month. If the CSM has no system for surfacing that signal, converting it to an expansion track, and handing it to the AE, the signal evaporates. The accounts that were ready for an expansion conversation in April won't be in the same position in June. *Goal-pursuit friction: Expansion Identification, Goal 3.*

**4. Incomplete handoffs from Sales that make onboarding harder.**
The closed-won deal arrives with a contract but without the stakeholder map, the real buying reason, or the verbal commitments the champion made during the sales cycle. The CSM has to reconstruct the account context from scratch while simultaneously running the onboarding. The result is a slower time to first value and a weaker first impression. Gains Grow Retain research consistently identifies handoff completeness as one of the strongest predictors of early-stage churn risk. *Goal-pursuit friction: Value Delivery and QBR (onboarding phase), Goal 4 and cross-functional bridge to Revenue Generator.*

**5. Renewal pressure from accounts that were never truly onboarded.**
Accounts that hit the first renewal with a weak product adoption footprint are the renewal conversations nobody wants. The CSM is left defending value that wasn't established at onboarding. The root cause is either incomplete handoff from Sales, a slow time to first value, or insufficient check-ins in the first 90 days, all of which the CSM either inherited or could have caught. *Goal-pursuit friction: Renewal Execution, Goal 2.*

## 6. What would offend them

**Individual CSM performance comparisons.** A card that compares one CSM's QBR completion rate to another's, or flags which CSM has the most at-risk accounts by name, is a manager conversation, not an intelligence conversation. The Customer Advocate receives portfolio-level and segment-level intelligence about their own book or the team's aggregate patterns, not rep-vs-rep comparisons.

**Compensation, OTE, or variable commission data.** The CSM's renewal quota and commission structure is an HR and Revenue Leader conversation. Surfacing OTE or comp-plan data to the Customer Advocate is out of scope.

**Health score as a single-score verdict.** The practitioner community is vocal that single-score health readings (green/yellow/red) mask more than they reveal. A card that says "42 of your 47 accounts are green" as a complete observation would be treated as superficial. The Customer Advocate wants to know what is moving inside the score, which component signals are changing, which accounts changed color, and what the health color distribution predicts about the renewal cohort.

**Individual customer name in a verdict frame.** Restating factual CRM data fields that happen to contain account names (e.g., "Lighthouse QBR completed April 22") is appropriate. Framing a named account as a problem, a risk, or a cautionary example is not. The card surfaces the signal; the human makes every judgment about what to do with it.

**Finance, GL, or compensation data.** Even though the CSM tracks renewal ARR, GL-level revenue recognition, deferred revenue schedules, and churn impact on financial statements are a Customer Leader and Finance conversation.

**Team capacity, headcount, or CSM-to-account-ratio recommendations.** How many CSMs to hire, how to restructure the book, and what the right CSM-to-account ratio is are Customer Leader and People decisions. The Customer Advocate receives intelligence about their own book, not workforce planning recommendations.

## 7. Five "that's me" quotes they would say

**Quote 1:** "I had 9 accounts where the champion went quiet for more than two weeks in April. We re-engaged all 9. Seven of them crossed back to green within 21 days. That's the whole job, catch it before the contract review."

**Quote 2:** "We hit 88% QBR completion on the strategic tier in April, up from 64% in February. But the number I actually care about is whether we captured a value story on each one. We did. That's what closes the renewal conversation."

**Quote 3:** "The handoff from Sales tells me everything. If I get a deal with a real stakeholder map, the actual buying reason, and the verbal commitments they made, I can onboard in 41 days. If I get a contract and a Salesforce record with three fields filled in, I'm starting from zero."

**Quote 4:** "Seven accounts crossed the 80% feature adoption ceiling this quarter. Those are expansion conversations, not renewal conversations. The AE and I should be talking about next-tier pricing before those accounts ask us about it."

**Quote 5:** "The Lighthouse QBR on April 22 went from three contacts to six, including the COO and two business-unit leads. That account is no longer a single-thread renewal risk. Buying committees that grow during the renewal window close 28 points higher."

## 8. Signal shapes worth distinguishing

The Customer Advocate's book produces signals that look related but answer different questions. Cards must respect that separation.

**P-CA-01 (book-wide multi-thread depth at 4+ contacts) is distinct from P-CA-10 (expansion-track multi-thread depth at 5+ contacts).** Both are relationship-depth signals, but they operate at different thresholds and serve different goals. P-CA-01 tracks whether accounts have reached the minimum multi-contact depth associated with renewal health (4+ contacts at 91% renewal vs. 67% at 1-2 contacts). P-CA-10 tracks whether accounts have crossed the deeper engagement level associated with expansion readiness (5+ contacts, 30% expand in the next 12 months vs. 5% at single-thread). The 4-contact threshold is a renewal protection signal; the 5-contact threshold is an expansion signal. They happen to be related, both measure multi-threading, but they answer different CSM questions and belong on different cards.

**P-CA-02 (champion re-engagement events) is distinct from P-CA-03 (stakeholder map freshness).** Both are relationship health signals, but they measure different relationship-coverage dimensions. P-CA-02 is an event signal: a specific champion contact had gone 14+ days without a logged interaction and was re-engaged in the measured period. The unit is a re-engagement event. P-CA-03 is a currency signal: the stakeholder map as a whole was updated within the last 30 days. A champion re-engagement is one interaction with one person; a fresh stakeholder map means the full contact picture is current. An account can have a fresh stakeholder map without any silent-champion event (because outreach has been consistent) or a silent-champion event without a stale stakeholder map (because the champion was one of several contacts documented recently). They are distinct relationship health dimensions.

**P-CA-09 (expansion-ready signals based on feature-adoption ceiling) is distinct from P-CA-11 (usage spike signals based on week-over-week engagement rate).** Both are expansion precursor signals, but they trigger on different data surfaces and carry different urgency windows. P-CA-09 fires when an account crosses 80% of its licensed feature set, a structural ceiling signal that says the account has absorbed what they bought and needs more. The signal is durable: an account at 80% adoption stays there until they expand or reduce their footprint. P-CA-11 fires when an account crosses a 40% week-over-week increase in active users or feature engagement, an event signal that says something changed in how the account is using the product. The signal is perishable: usage spikes correlate with a 33% faster expansion close-cycle when the CSM acts on them quickly, but the signal window is short. One is a structural readiness signal; the other is a behavioral urgency signal.

**P-CA-12 (QBR completion with value story captured) is distinct from P-CA-13 (time-to-value on new onboardings).** Both are value-delivery signals, but they operate at different lifecycle stages. P-CA-12 is an ongoing relationship signal: strategic-tier accounts completing QBRs with a documented value story in CRM, an indicator of how well the CSM is managing the health-review rhythm for accounts that are already live and have been through the onboarding process. P-CA-13 is a new-account signal: how quickly Q2 onboarding cohorts reached their first activated success criterion, an indicator of onboarding quality and Sales handoff completeness for net-new customers. One measures value demonstration in the ongoing relationship; the other measures value speed in the new-account motion. The two can diverge independently, excellent QBR completion can coexist with slow onboarding, and vice versa.

## 9. Organizational Integration

**Upward (what the CSM's work must visibly serve):**
The Customer Advocate's outcomes flow into the Customer Leader's NRR forecast, GRR reporting, and expansion pipeline visibility. Every QBR completion, early-renewal commit, and expansion track sourced by the CSM is raw material for the Customer Leader's board narrative. The Customer Advocate serves the CS organization's proof point that relationships and revenue compound together, the CSM does not need to think about board narrative, but their work must be visible, logged, and legible to the system above them.

**Downward (what the Customer Advocate directs and is accountable for):**
At the IC level, the Customer Advocate has no direct reports. Their accountability flows through the accounts: every account in their book is a system they are responsible for keeping healthy. The CSM owns the CRM record, the health score inputs they can influence, the QBR cadence, the stakeholder map currency, and the relationship with the champion. They delegate nothing formally, but they coordinate everything through the account.

**Horizontal (dependencies and handoffs):**
- Revenue Generator (AE): The closest and most frequent cross-functional dependency. The CSM receives the closed-won account from the AE at handoff, needs the handoff package to be complete (stakeholder map, real buying reason, verbal commitments), and sources expansion opportunities back to the AE when usage signals surface. The quality of the AE-to-CSM handoff and the speed of the CSM-to-AE expansion pass are both tracked signals.
- Marketing Leader / Marketing Strategist: The CSM is the source of the advocate pipeline, accounts willing to do case studies, reference calls, or community participation. Marketing sources advocates from the CS book through the CSM. The CSM surfaces candidates; Marketing owns the program and activation.
- Customer Operator: The CSM depends on the CS platform infrastructure, Gainsight health score models, ChurnZero playbook triggers, CRM sync accuracy, that the Customer Operator builds and maintains. The CSM does not configure the system but relies on it daily. Health score model recalibration, QBR workflow changes, and CRM field additions that affect the CSM's daily workflow all require the Customer Operator.
- Customer Technician: The CSM hands off new accounts to the Customer Technician (or shares the onboarding motion with them) for technical implementation and integration. The CSM retains the relationship and renewal accountability; the Technician owns the technical delivery. When onboarding stalls, the CSM is the point of escalation.
